Question 1
(a) Total product = Labour × Average product P = 4 × 18.4 = 73.6 bags Average product = Total product / Labour Q = 97.6 / 9 = 10.8 bags Marginal product = Δ Total product / Δ Labour R = (103.8 - 96) / (7 - 6) = 7.8 (b)(i) Increasing returns began at the very first unit of labour. i.e. labour = 1 (b)(ii) Negative returns began at the 9th unit of labour. i.e. labour = 9 (c) The relationship between the Average product and the marginal product is one of direct proportion. An increment in one results in an increment in the other and vice-versa.
Question 2
(a)(i) Variable Costs: Leather, Glue, Nails, Rubber soles, Diesel, Wages V.C = 200 + 50 + 20 + 80 + 120 + 250 = $720.00 (a)(ii) Fixed Costs: Electricity(25) + Telephone(15) + Insurance(5) + Rent(50) + Advert(15) + Salary(350) + Equipment(100) + Tools(60) + Power(250) Σ FC = $870.00 (a)(iii) Total Cost = 720 + 870 = $1590 (b)(i) Average cost per pair = 1590 / 250 = $6.36 (b)(ii) Total Revenue = 250 × 10 = $2500 | Profit = $2500 - 1590 = $910.00 (c) Since average revenue is greater than average variable cost, the firm should continue production in the short run.
Question 3
(3a) A capitalist economic system is one where private individuals or entities own and control property and the means of production, and where market forces like supply and demand determine prices and resource allocation. (3b) (i) Economic Freedom and Choice: Capitalism emphasizes freedom for individuals to choose what to consume, produce, and invest in. (ii) Economic Growth and Efficiency: The system encourages competition and self-interest, which can lead to greater innovation, efficiency, and overall economic growth. (iii) Limited Government Intervention: Capitalism typically involves a limited role for the government, primarily to protect private property rights. (3c) (i) Addressing Inequality: Pure capitalism can lead to significant wealth concentration. A mixed economy uses government policies to redistribute wealth. (ii) Providing Public Goods: A mixed economy allows for government intervention to provide essential services and protect consumers/environment.
Question 4
(4a) Hoarding is the practice of accumulating and storing large quantities of goods to create artificial scarcity. Rationing is a government-imposed policy of controlling distribution and limiting purchases during shortages. (4b) Functions of a Wholesaler: (i) Buys goods in large quantities directly from manufacturers. (ii) Breaks bulk and sells goods in smaller quantities to retailers. (iii) Provides storage facilities (warehousing) for goods. (iv) Offers transportation services to deliver goods to retailers. (v) Provides market information to manufacturers. (4c) Problems of Distribution: (i) Inadequate Infrastructure: Poor roads and power supply hinder efficient movement. (ii) High Transportation Costs: Inefficient routes and fuel prices increase final product prices. (iii) Inefficient Inventory Management: Inaccurate forecasting leads to overstocking or shortages. (iv) Lack of Supply Chain Visibility: Absence of real-time tracking leads to delays.
Question 5
(5a) Difference between Food Crop & Cash Crop Farming: (i) Food crops are for direct consumption; Cash crops are for sale/export. (ii) Food crops feed local population; Cash crops provide raw materials/forex. (iii) Food crop farming is often small-scale; Cash crop farming is large-scale/commercial. (5b) Characteristics of Plantation Agriculture: (i) Use of modern machines (tractors, harvesters). (ii) Large expanse of land cultivated. (iii) High capital investment required. (iv) Production on a commercial scale. (5c) Contribution of Industry to Agriculture: (i) Supply of farm inputs/machinery (fertilizers, tractors). (ii) Development of storage/preservation tech (silos, cold rooms). (iii) Creation of markets for agro-products (raw materials for factories). (iv) Employment generation for rural communities.
Question 6
(6a) Features of Underdeveloped Countries: (i) Low per capita income: Low earnings lead to poverty and limited savings. (ii) High population growth: High birth rates pressure social services. (iii) Heavy dependence on agriculture: Reliance on subsistence farming. (iv) Poor industrial development: Weak industrial sectors and low productivity. (6b) Ways to Finance Development: (i) Taxation: Direct and indirect taxes. (ii) Foreign loans/grants: Assistance from World Bank, IMF. (iii) Public borrowing: Treasury bills, bonds. (iv) Savings from export earnings: Revenue from oil, cocoa, etc.
Question 7
(7a) An economic union is a trade bloc composed of a customs union and a common market. (7b) World Bank vs IMF: (i) Function: World Bank focuses on long-term development/poverty. IMF focuses on short-term stability/balance of payments. (ii) Target: World Bank lends to developing countries. IMF assists any member in crisis. (iii) Funding: World Bank uses bonds/contributions. IMF uses quotas. (7c) Functions of IMF: (i) Providing financial assistance (loans). (ii) Facilitating international trade (exchange stability). (iii) Offering policy advice and technical assistance. (iv) Monitoring the global economy.
Question 8
(8a) Solid mineral resources are naturally occurring inorganic substances found in the earth’s crust (gold, coal, tin) with economic value. (8b) Benefits: (i) Source of revenue and foreign exchange. (ii) Industrial development (raw materials). (iii) Employment opportunities (mining jobs). (8c) How to Promote Sector: (i) Provision of incentives (tax holidays). (ii) Development of infrastructure (power, roads). (iii) Favourable policies (laws restricting export of unprocessed minerals).
